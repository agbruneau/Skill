# Bible Translation Reference

A guide to choosing your `BIBLE_TRANSLATION` value, organized for a **Reformed Baptist + John MacArthur** context. Translations are tiered by suitability for expository preaching in this tradition, not alphabetically.

---

## Tier 1 — Recommended for Expository Preaching

These translations are word-for-word (formal equivalence), preserve the literary structure of the original, and are the standard choices in serious Reformed and Reformed Baptist pulpits.

| Abbreviation | Full Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **LSB** | Legacy Standard Bible (2021) | The translation of choice at Master's Seminary and Grace Community Church. A revision of the NASB 1995 by Master's Seminary faculty (John MacArthur, Abner Chou, et al.) emphasizing maximum lexical consistency: every Hebrew/Greek word is rendered the same English term wherever possible, the divine name is rendered "Yahweh" rather than "LORD," "slave" replaces "servant" where the Greek is *doulos*. **Default recommendation when working in MacArthur's stream.** |
| **NASB 1995** | New American Standard Bible (1995 edition) | The translation MacArthur preached from for decades before the LSB. Strict word-for-word; the most literal mainstream English Bible before the LSB. The 2020 update is more readable but loosens some literalness; many MacArthur-aligned pastors stayed on 1995 or moved to LSB. |
| **ESV** | English Standard Version (2001, rev. 2016) | The de facto standard in contemporary Reformed circles (Crossway, TGC, Desiring God, 9Marks, T4G). Word-for-word, more literary than NASB, slightly less literal. Strong study Bible ecosystem (ESV Study Bible, Reformation Study Bible). Recommended if LSB/NASB feels overly stiff. |
| **KJV** | King James Version (1611, with 1769 standardization) | The translation of Spurgeon, Owen, Edwards, the Puritans, and historic Reformed Baptist preaching. Underlying NT text is the *Textus Receptus*; OT is the Masoretic. Still the standard in confessional 1689 LBCF Reformed Baptist congregations and Independent Baptist churches. Beautiful, weighty, occasionally archaic. |
| **NKJV** | New King James Version (1982) | KJV updated into modern English while keeping the *Textus Receptus* base and the formal style. Common in traditional Baptist and some Reformed Baptist churches that want KJV substance with modern readability. |

## Tier 2 — Acceptable for Preaching with Caveats

| Abbreviation | Full Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **CSB** | Christian Standard Bible (2017) | Published by B&H (Southern Baptist). "Optimal equivalence" — slightly less literal than ESV, more readable. Doctrinally reliable. Suitable for pulpit use; less precise than Tier 1 for word studies. |
| **NET** | New English Translation | Functional equivalence with 60,000+ scholarly translator notes (free at [netbible.org](https://netbible.org)). Useful as a study companion alongside a Tier 1 translation; the notes are the real value. Not typical for pulpit reading. |

## Tier 3 — Use with Discernment

These translations are widely available but carry editorial choices that conservative Reformed pastors typically push back on. Use for cross-reference or audience accommodation, not as the primary preaching text.

| Abbreviation | Full Name | Why discernment is needed |
|---|---|---|
| **NIV** (2011) | New International Version | The 2011 revision introduced gender-inclusive language ("brothers and sisters" where the Greek is *adelphoi*, plural "they" for generic singulars), reversing the position the NIV held in the late 1990s when the TNIV controversy first surfaced. Many conservative Reformed and Reformed Baptist pastors moved off the NIV at that point. The 1984 NIV (no longer in print) does not have this issue but is hard to source. |
| **NLT** | New Living Translation | Thought-for-thought paraphrase. Smooths idioms and theological vocabulary in ways that obscure the underlying terms (propitiation, justification, sanctification). Useful for outreach reading; not for exposition. |
| **MSG** | The Message | Eugene Peterson's paraphrase. Often takes interpretive liberties that flatten or invert the doctrinal force of a passage (e.g., the Lord's Prayer, Romans 12:1-2). MacArthur and others have publicly criticized it. **Do not use for preaching.** |
| **AMP** | Amplified Bible | Brackets multiple senses into the text, which can read as if every word carries every meaning at once. Useful as a personal study aid, not as a pulpit Bible. |
| **TPT** | The Passion Translation | Brian Simmons. Theologically charismatic, methodologically loose, with insertions that have no manuscript basis. **Avoid.** |
| **CEB, NRSV, NRSVue** | Common English Bible / New Revised Standard Version | Mainline Protestant translations. NRSV is rigorous academically but reflects mainline editorial choices on gender and on some doctrinally-loaded terms. Not standard in Reformed Baptist circles. |

## Original Languages

| Resource | Use |
|---|---|
| **NA28 / UBS5** (Greek NT) | Standard critical text. Used by all Tier 1 translations except KJV/NKJV (which use the *Textus Receptus*). |
| **BHS / BHQ** (Hebrew Bible) | Standard Hebrew critical text (Masoretic). |
| **LXX** (Septuagint) | Greek translation of the OT, used heavily by NT writers. Cite when a NT quotation matches the LXX rather than the Masoretic. |

## French Reformed Translations

The user of this skill is francophone. If skill output ever needs to cite or quote scripture in French, default to a French translation in the Reformed tradition rather than transliterating the English.

| Abbreviation | Full Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **LSG** | Louis Segond 1910 | The classic French Reformed translation. The "KJV equivalent" in francophone Reformed and Baptiste circles. Domaine public, freely available. |
| **NEG 1979** | Nouvelle Édition de Genève | A modest revision of LSG keeping the same Reformed lineage. Standard in many francophone Réformé and Baptiste évangélique churches. |
| **S21** | Segond 21 (2007) | Modern revision of the Segond, more readable while staying formally equivalent. Widely used in francophone evangelical churches today. Published by the Société Biblique de Genève. |
| **Darby (FR)** | Bible J.N. Darby (1885, rev.) | French translation by J.N. Darby. Highly literal, dispensationalist heritage (Darby is a foundational figure for the dispensational tradition MacArthur stands in). Excellent for word-for-word study; archaic. |
| **KJF** | King James Française | French rendering of the KJV; *Textus Receptus* base. Used in some confessional Baptist circles. |
| **BJC** | Bible de Jérusalem / TOB | Catholic / mainline ecumenical translations. Useful for comparison; not standard in Réformé evangelical preaching. |

For pulpit use in francophone Reformed Baptist contexts: **S21** for accessibility, **NEG 1979** or **Darby** for greater formal precision, **LSG** for classic weight.

---

## How to Choose

- **MacArthur stream, English preaching:** **LSB** is the explicit recommendation. NASB 1995 if LSB unavailable.
- **Broader Reformed / Reformed Baptist, English preaching:** **ESV** is the contemporary default. KJV/NKJV if your congregation is confessional 1689 / traditional.
- **Francophone Reformed Baptist preaching:** **S21** for the pulpit, **Darby** or **NEG 1979** for study and exposition.
- **For deep word studies:** stay in Tier 1 (LSB, NASB 1995, ESV, KJV) and check the original languages directly.
- **For evangelism or accessibility readings only** (not the primary preaching text): NLT or CSB are acceptable; never MSG, TPT, or AMP for preaching.

If `BIBLE_TRANSLATION` is left unset, the default is **LSB**.
